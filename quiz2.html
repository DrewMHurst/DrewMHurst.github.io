Introduction

This learning module will focus on the different parts of speech within the English language. There are eight grammatical parts of speech: nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections.

A part of speech is a category to which a word is assigned in a sentence by its syntactic meaning. Each part of speech explains not what the word is, but how it is used. In fact, many words can function as more than one part of speech. Understanding how words function in a sentence, paragraph, and an essay will improve your speaking and writing skills making you an effective communicator.

Nouns

A noun names something, such as a person, place, thing, or idea. In essence, everything we can see or talk about is illustrated by a word, and that word is a noun. All nouns can fall into one of two categories: common nouns and proper nouns.

Examples
  • I see a boy.
  • Look at those pigs.
  • It is another day.

Proper nouns are specific terms for a person, place, things or ideas.

Examples:
  • I see Josh.
  • Look at the Empire State Building.
  • This is Kansas City.
  • Today is Tuesday.

Proper nouns should always be capitalize in a sentence.

Nouns can be classified into concrete or abstract.

Concrete nouns are everything we can experience through our five senses (i.e. see, feel, smell, taste, or hear).

Examples:
  • I see a bird.
  • I feel a feather.
  • I smell roses.
  • I hear fire works.

Whereas abstract nouns are things we cannot perceive with our five senses. Abstract nouns are ideas.

Examples
  • I am sad.
  • He is brave.
  • They will recieve justice.
  • Today is Saturday.

Note: Abstract nouns are rarely proper nouns.

Pronouns

A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun. Usually the pronoun substitutes for a specific noun.

Examples
  • Susie laughed so hard, milk came out of Susie nose.
  • Susie laughed so hard, milk came out of her nose.

There are seven different types of pronouns: Personal, possessive, reflective, relative, demostrative, indefinite, and interrogative. While there are tens of thousands of nouns, there are only about one hundred pronouns. The table below list the majority of pronouns in the english language.

Verbs

A verb is a word that express action or a state of being. In a sentence, verbs describe what the subject is doing or being.

Examples
  • Katlin runs home.
  • Drew found his keys.
  • Run! (In this format the verb, run, is a complete sentence.)

The examples above show the verb as doing some sort of "action," but verbs can also be used to describe a subject state of being in a sentence. These verbs are called Linking verbs; all linking verbs are state of being.

Examples
  • I am happy.
  • My name is Mary Jane.
  • We are friends.

In many sentences, verbs work together. In these cases helping verbs are usually followed by a main verbs.

Examples
  • I am driving a truck.
  • I did not empty the trash.
  • You ought to be with me. (Title of Al Green's 1972 song.)

There are 24 helping verbs in the English language: forms of to have, do and be, which may function as main verbs; and ten modals, which function only as helping verbs. All helping verbs are listed in the table below.

Adjectives

An adjective is a word used to describe, or modify, a noun or pronoun.

Examples
  • Look at the black sheep.
  • He looks upset.
  • Mike had sixteen candles.

Adjectives can be placed before a noun, which is called and attribute position, or after a noun, which is called a predictive position.

Examples
  • This is my broken washing machine.
  • My washing machine is broken.

Another type of adjective are the articles. There are two type of articles: definite and indefinite.

The definite article is the word "the." It is used to define that a noun is sppecific.

Examples
  • Look at the queen.
  • Can we go to the park?
  • I love the poorly educated.

The indefinite article is the word "a" or "an." It is used to determine that something is non-specific or generic.

Examples
  • I am a king.
  • That is an owl.
  • I love the poorly educated.

The indefinite article "a" is used with nouns that start with consonates or consonate sounding nouns, whereas the indefinite article "an" is used with nouns that start with a vowel or vowel sounding nouns.

Note: Both definite and indefinite articles always start before the noun they modify.

Adverb

An adverb is a word used to modify, or qualify, a verb, adjecive or another adverb. In general, adverbs modify everything that is not a noun. Adverbs usually describe how, where or when an action takes place.

Examples
  • He grab the rope harshly. (Grab how?)
  • Holly regularly sings at the Holiday Inn. (Sings when?)
  • You can place that here. (Place where?)

Adverbs can be placed before or after the verb they modify, whereas for adjectives they must be placed in front.

Examples
  • George is extremely upleasent.
  • They are working effieciently.

Adverbs can also modify other adverbs.

Examples
  • She thinks very highly of you. (Very modifies highly, which modifies the verb think.)
  • You can place that over here. (Over is modifying the adverb here.)

The negators not and never are classified as adverbs. A word such as cannot contains the helping verb can and the adverb not. This is also true fore the word can't, which contains a contracted form of the adverb not.

Prepositions

A preposition is a word placed before a noun, pronoun and phrase to other words within a sentence. In general, prepositions establishes relationship of how/for what, when or where.

Examples
  • This paintbrush is for drawing. (What is this paintbrush used for?)
  • After Susie purchase the red dress, she went home. (When did she purchase the dress?)
  • The box is under the table. (Where is the box?)

Prepositional phrases consist of a preposition and a noun; however, the prepositional phrase is not the subject of a sentence. They can act as nouns, adjectives and adverbs within a sentence.

Examples
  • Lilth played the voilin with the grace of an angel. (In this sentece there are 2 prepositional phrases, with the grace and of an angel, both acting as adverbs to modify the verb, played.)

Conjunctions

conjunctions join words, phrases, or clauses (both dependent and independent), and they indicate the relation between the elements that are joined. There are three types of conjunctions: coordinating, correlative, and subordinating.

Coordinating conjunctions joins elements of equal grammatical rank, such as two verbs, two nouns, two adjectives, two phases, or independent clauses. There are seven coordinating conjunctions: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so.

Examples
  • This paintbrush is for drawing. (What is this paintbrush used for?)
  • After Susie purchase the red dress, she went home. (When did she purchase the dress?)
  • The box is under the table. (Where is the box?)

Subordinating conjunctions are words or phrases that joins a dependent clause with an independent clause. The subordinates can start at the beginning or middle of a sentence.

Examples
  • Although it is raining, we had fun all day.
  • I paid Larry because I owed him the money.

There are many words and phrases that can be used as subordinating prepositions, but here are a few examples of the most common: Although, After, Before, Because, How, If, Since, When, Whereas.

Correlative conjunctions work in pairs to join words, phrases, or clauses. The correlative conjunctions are either/or, neither/nor, both/and.

Examples
  • Either Sammie or Billy have my keys.
  • Neither Sue nor Sara will help you with your homework.

Interjections

An interjection is a word used to demostrate emotion, feeling or suprise. Interjections are grammatically independent phrase that can stand by themselves, but they are often contained within larger sentences. If by themselves, they are usually followed with an exclamation mark.

Examples
  • Ahh, that feels great.
  • Congrats!
  • Wow!
  • Well duh!
  • Yikes I didn't know that school was today!
  • I lost my wallet, dang!
