Understanding Exponents

An exponent refers to how many times a number is multiplied by itself. For example, if the number 3 is multiplied by itself four times, we would write this as follows:

$$3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 81$$

We can rewrite the above expression as 34, which is the proper math notation for an exponential expression. In this example, 4 is called the Exponent or Power and 3 is the Base that is being multiplied by itself four times.

Here we have the example 34 represented as our exponential expression, but this holds true for any number whether 510 or 500100. Instead of using specific numbers, lets write an exponential expression with arbitrary values representing any number:

Let's do more examples:

Examples:
  • \[ 3^{2} = 3 \times 3 = 9 \]
  • $$ 2^{3} = 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8 $$
  • $$ 5^{5} = 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 3,125 $$

Note: All the examples above can be multiply by the number 1 and not have it change the output value because 1 act as an identity.

Examples:
  • $$ 3^{2} = 1 \times 3 \times 3 = 9 $$
  • $$ 2^{3} = 1 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8 $$
  • $$ 5^{5} = 1 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 3,125 $$

What if we power a base number to 1 and 0?

$$ 2^{1} = 1 \times 2 = 2 $$ $$ 2^{0} = 1 = 1 $$

Whenever an exponential expression has a 1 for it's power, it will always be equal to the base number. Subsequently, whenever an exponential expression has a 0 for it's power, it will always be equal to 1.

Identity Exponent Rule

$$ A^{ 1} = A $$

and

$$ A^{ 0} = 1 $$

Where A represents any base number.

Zero to the power of zero, 00, does not have an agreed upon value in mathematics; however for introductory to intermediate algebra, it has been agreed that 00 = 1.

Multiplying Exponents

We can multiply exponential expressions with different numbers.

Examples:
  • $$ 3 \times 5^{3} = 3 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 375 $$
  • $$ 4^{2} \times 6 = 4 \times 4 \times 6 = 96 $$
  • $$ 2^{4} \times 3^{2} = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 144 $$

As stated earlier, we can also write the base number as a variable with letters (or symbols) standing in for numbers.

Examples:
  • $$ a \times c \times c \times c = a c^{3} $$
  • <01> b \times b \times a \times a \times a \times a = b^{2} a^{4} $$
  • $$ a \times a \times b \times b \times b \times b \times y = a^{2}b^{4} y $$

When multiplying exponential expressions with the same base, we add the exponents together.

Examples:
  • $$ c^{ 8 } c = c^{8} \times c = c^{8+1} = c^{9} $$
  • $$ y^{ 5 } y y = y^{5+1+1} = y^{7} $$
  • $$ 7^{ 4 } 7^{ 2 } 7 = 7^{4+2+1} = 7^{7} = 823,543 $$

Adding exponents together if they share the same base can be written in the following formula:

Product Rule

$$ A^{ k_{1} } \times A^{ k_{2} } = A^{k_{1}+k_{2}} $$

Where A represents the base number or variable. k1 and k2 represent the exponents.

Note:k1 and k2 can equal each other or zero.

What if the bases aren't the same? What do we do in those situations?

Examples:
  • $$ 4^{2} 7^{3} 4^{3} = ? $$
  • $$ e^{ 3} e^{ 2 } c^{ 4 } = ? $$
  • $$ a^{ 4 } y a y^{ 3 } = ? $$
  • $$ 4X^{ 4 }5b^{ 2 } = ? $$

As stated previously, you only add exponents that have the same base.

Examples:
  • $$ 4^{2} 7^{3} 4^{3} = 4^{2+3} 7^{3} = 4^{5} \times 7^{3} = 1024 \times 343 = 351,232 $$
  • $$ e^{ 3} e^{ 2 } c^{ 4 } = e^{3+2} c^{4} = e^{5} c^{4} $$
  • $$ a^{ 4 } y a y^{ 3 } = a^{4+1} y^{3+1} = a^{5} y^{4} $$
  • $$ 4X^{ 4 }5 b^{ 2 } = 4 \times 5 X^{ 4 } b^{ 2 } = 20X^{ 4 } b^{ 2 }$$

Division and Negative Exponents

In division, exponents are subtracted from each other provided the base are the same as shown in the examples below:

Examples:
  • $$ c^{ 4 } \div c^{ 2 } \Longrightarrow \frac{c^{4}}{c^{2}} = \frac{c \times c \times \cancel{c \times c}}{\cancel{c \times c}} \Longrightarrow c^{4-2} = c^{2} $$
  • $$ X^{ 9 } \div X^{ 6 } \Longrightarrow \frac{X^{9}}{X^{6}} = X^{9-6} = X^{3} $$
  • $$ 4^{ 6 } \div 4^{ 5 } = \frac{4^{6}}{4^{5}} = 4^{6-5} = 4^{1} = 4 $$
  • $$ 3 a^{ 3 } \div 5 a = \frac{3 a^{3}}{5 a} = \frac{3}{5}a^{3-1} = \frac{3}{5}a^{2} $$

We can write a general formula for dividing exponents.

Quotient Rule

$$ A^{ k_{1} } \div A^{ k_{2} } = \frac{A^{k_{1}}}{A^{k_{2}}} = x^{1} $$ $\[x^2 + y^2 = z^2\]$

Where A represents the base number or variable that is not equal to zero. k1 and k2 are exponents.

What happens when the denomenator's exponent is greater than the numerator's exponent? The same division formula still applies; the only difference is that the exponent will be negative as shown below:

$$ b^{4} \div b^{6} = \frac{b^{4}}{b^{6}} = b^{4-6} = b^{-2} = \frac{1}{ b^{2} } $$

Any number or variable with a negative exponent equals its reciprocal with the exponent turned to positive as the formula below shows.

Negative Exponent Identity

$$ A^{-k} = \frac{1}{A^{k}} $$

Where A is not equal to zero.

Examples
  • $$ 7^{-2} = \frac{1}{7^{2}} = \frac{1}{49} $$
  • $$ 4^{3} \div 4^{5} = \frac{4^{3}}{4^{5}} = 4^{3-5} = 4^{-2} = \frac{1}{ 4^{2}} = \frac{1}{16} $$
  • $$ 3 X^{ 7 } \div 6 X^{9} = \frac{3 X^{7}}{6 X^{9}} =kj \frac{1 X^{7}}{2 X^{9}} = \frac{1}{2}X^{7-9} = \frac{1}{2}X^{-2} = \frac{1}{2X^{2}} $$

Powering Exponents

In this learning module, we have discussed multiplying and divinding exponents, but what if we were to power and exponential as shown:

$$ {(3^{5})}^{3} $$

The way to solving these types of problems is to go back to the basic understanding of what an exponent is. An exponent tells us how many times to multiply a number or variable to itself. For this example we need to multiply 35 three times using the exponential product rule.

$$ {(3^{5})}^{3} = 3^{5} \times 3^{5} \times 3^{5} = 3^{5+5+5} = 3^{15} $$

The answer is 315, but we could have obtained this answer faster if we just multiply the inner and outer exponents, 3 and 5, together.

$$ {(3^{5})}^{3} = 3^{5 \times 3} = 3^{15} $$

The Power rule for exponents states that when an exponential expression is raised to another exponent, you simply combine the exponents into one by multiplying them.

Power Rule

$$ (A^{k_1})^{k_2} = A^{k_1 \times k_2} $$

Where Ak1 is the exponential expression and k2 is the exponent.

Examples:
  • $$ (X^{ 2 })^{ 7 } = X^{ 2 \times 7 } = X^{ 14 } $$
  • $$ (c^{ 4 })^{ 2 } = c^{ 4 \times 2 } = c^{ 8 } $$
  • $$ ((2t)^{ 5 })^{ 3 } =(2^{ 5 } \times t^{ 5 })^{ 3 } = 2^{ 5 \times 3 } \times t^{ 5 \times 3 } = 2^{ 15 } \times t^{ 15 } = 32,768t^{ 15 } $$
  • $$ ( \frac{a^{3}}{b} )^{ 5 } = ({a}^{3}{b}^{-1})^{5} = {a}^{3 \times 5 }{b}^{-1 \times 5 } = {a}^{ 15 }{b}^{ -5 } = \frac{a^{15}}{b^{ 5 }} $$

Introduction to Radicals

Part One

Up to now we've only discussed exponents as whole number integers, but exponents can also be fractions and irrational numbers. For example, 41/2, 81/3, and 161/4 are all exponential fractions.

An integer exponent tells us how many times to multiply a base number whereas a fractional exponent that is less than 1 tells us to reduce the base number or variable.

Examples:
  • $$ 4^{ \frac{1}{2}} $$

    the exponent tells us to reduce the base number, 4, by one half, which equals:

    $$ \cancel{ 2 } \times 2 = 2 $$
  • $$ 8^{ \frac{1}{3}} $$

    the exponent tells us to reduce the base number, 8, by one third, which equals:

    $$ \cancel{ 2 } \times \cancel{ 2 } \times 2 = 2 $$
  • $$ 16^{ \frac{1}{4}} $$

    the exponent tells us to reduce the base number, 16, by one fourth, which equals:

    $$ \cancel{ 2 } \times \cancel{ 2 } \times \cancel{ 2 } \times 2 = 2 $$

The answer,2, is called the root or radical number for all three of these following examples. When an exponent is written a a fraction and we are determining the root of a base, we call the fractional exponential a radical expression and write them in a different mathematical form as the image below shows:

For radical expressions we call the base a radicand and the radical sign indicates that the root will be taken of the radicand. The denominator exponent is called the index and tells to what degree the root will be taken to.

Examples:
  • $$ 4^{ \frac{1}{2} } \Rightarrow \sqrt[2]{4} $$

    square root of 4

  • $$ 8^{ \frac{1}{3} } \Rightarrow \sqrt[3]{8} $$

    cube root of 8

  • $$ 16^{ \frac{1}{4} } \Rightarrow \sqrt[4]{16} $$

    fourth root of 16

When writing a radical with a square root, in general, the index number is not included because it is commonly understood.

Examples:
  • $$ \sqrt[]{9} = \sqrt[]{ \cancel{3} \times 3 } = 3 $$
  • $$ \sqrt[]{25} = \sqrt[]{ \cancel{5} \times 5 } = 5 $$
  • $$ \sqrt[3]{125} = \sqrt[3]{ \cancel{5} \times \cancel{5} \times 5} = 5 $$
  • $$ \sqrt[3]{64} = \sqrt[3]{ \cancel{4} \times \cancel{4} \times 4} = 4 $$

All of the examples thus shown have been radicals of integers with perfect squares, cubes or quadrants for their roots, but many integers don't have rational numbers as their roots. For example, the square root of 2, 3 and 5 are all irrational numbers. For this reason we only show these numbers square roots with the radical sign ( √ 2, √ 3 and √ 5 ) and not as their approximate values, which are 1.41, 1.73 and 2.24 respectively.

Although most integer's roots will not be a rational number, many integers will have roots that are the products/multiplications of a rational and irrational number.

Examples:
  • $$ \sqrt[]{8} = \sqrt[]{ 4 \times 2 } = \sqrt[]{(2 \times 2) \times 3} \Rightarrow (2 \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt[]{2} = 2 \sqrt[]{2} $$
  • $$ \sqrt[]{12} = \sqrt[]{ 4 \times 3 } = \sqrt[]{(2 \times 2) \times 3} \Rightarrow (2 \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt[]{3} = 2 \sqrt[]{3} $$
  • $$ \sqrt[]{32} = \sqrt[]{ 16 \times 2 } = \sqrt[]{(4 \times 4) \times 2} \Rightarrow (4 \times \cancel{4}) \sqrt[]{2} = 4 \sqrt[]{2} $$
  • $$ \sqrt[]{50} = \sqrt[]{ 25 \times 2 } = \sqrt[]{(5 \times 5) \times 2} \Rightarrow (5 \times \cancel{5}) \sqrt[]{2} = 5 \sqrt[]{2} $$
  • $$ \sqrt[]{120} = \sqrt[]{ 4 \times 30 } =\sqrt[]{(2 \times 2) \times 5 \times 6} \Rightarrow (2 \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt[]{5 \times 6} $$
  •           $$ = 2 \sqrt[]{5 \times 6} = 2 \sqrt[]{30} $$

To determine square roots of integers, use the factorization process you learned in arthmetic. If more help is needed, use a multiplications table.

Introduction to Radicals

Part Two

What about fractional exponents with numerators greater than 1 or a fraction that is greater than 1? For example, how do we write 43/2 as radical expression and find the root?

Examples:
  • $$ 4^{ \frac{3}{2} } = \sqrt{ 4^{3} } = \sqrt{4 \times 4 \times 4} = \sqrt{(2 \times 2) (2 \times 2) (2 \times 2)} $$
  •       $$ = (2 \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt{ (2 \times 2) (2 \times 2)} = 2 \times (2 \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt{ (2 \times 2)} $$
  •       $$ = 2 \times 2 \times (2 \times \cancel{2}) = 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8 $$

As the example above shows, we first mulitply the radicant then we find the root. For radical expressions, the numerator exponent is included with the radicant.

Let's do some more examples so you can get a better understanding in how to solve these types of problems.

Examples:
  • $$ 7^{ \frac{4}{3} } = \sqrt[3]{ 7^{4} } = \sqrt[3]{7 \times 7 \times 7 \times 7} = \sqrt[3]{(7 \times 7 \times 7) \times 7} $$
  •       $$ = (7 \times \cancel{7} \times \cancel{7}) \sqrt[3]{7} $$
  •       $$ = 7 \sqrt[3]{7} $$
  • $$ 8^{ \frac{2}{3} } = \sqrt[3]{ 8^{3} } = \sqrt[3]{8 \times 8} = \sqrt[3]{(2 \times 2 \times 2)(2 \times 2 \times 2)} $$
  •       $$ = (2 \times \cancel{2} \times \cancel{2}) \sqrt[3]{(2 \times 2 \times 2)} = 2 \times (2 \times \cancel{2} \times \cancel{2}) $$
  •       $$ = 2 \times 2 = 4 $$
  • $$ 10^{ \frac{5}{2} } = \sqrt{ 10^{5} } = \sqrt{ 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 } $$
  •         $$ = \sqrt{ (10 \times 10) (10 \times 10) \times 10 } = (10 \times \cancel{10}) \sqrt{ (10 \times 10) \times 10 } $$
  •         $$ = 10 \times (10 \times \cancel{10}) \sqrt{ 10 } = 10 \times 10 \sqrt{10} $$
  •         $$ = 100 \sqrt{ 10 } $$
